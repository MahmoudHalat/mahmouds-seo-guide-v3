# Psychology & Mental Models for Marketing Decisions

Load when: "why would someone do X", "behavioral", "persuasion", "nudge", "cognitive bias", "mental model for this decision", "how to ethically influence", "why isn't this converting psychologically", "decision framework", "customer is irrational".

Applied behavioral science — the models that actually show up in marketing decisions. Three buckets: biases that drive buying, mental models for strategy, and effects that shape memory and attention. End with ethical guardrails — these tools can manipulate, but the only durable strategy is aligning customer self-interest with product truth.

---

## 1. Core Behavioral Biases That Drive Buying Decisions

| Bias | Definition | Marketing Application | Concrete Tactic |
|------|-----------|----------------------|-----------------|
| **Loss aversion** | Losing $100 feels ~2x worse than gaining $100 feels good | Frame in terms of what they lose by not acting | "Stop wasting 4 hours/week on reporting" beats "save 4 hours/week" |
| **Social proof** | People infer correct behavior from others' behavior | Show customer count, logos, reviews, activity indicators | "Used by 12,000 teams" + 3 logos above fold; not "Used by thousands" |
| **Scarcity** | Limited supply raises perceived value | Time-bound or quantity-bound offers | "Only 8 spots left" with decrementing counter; NOT fake countdowns |
| **Anchoring** | First number shapes perception of subsequent numbers | Show a high-tier price first; strike-through MSRP | 3-tier pricing with middle tier highlighted — middle gets 60%+ selection |
| **Framing** | Identical facts read differently based on presentation | Reframe negatives as positives (or vice versa for competitors) | "95% success rate" > "5% failure rate"; "Save $120/yr" > "$10/mo" |
| **Reciprocity** | Receiving creates pressure to return the favor | Free tools, templates, audits before asking for the sale | Free calculator that emails results; free 10-page audit before pitching |
| **Commitment/Consistency** | Small yes leads to larger yes | Micro-commitments: email > trial > paid | "Get your free score" → "see full report" → "start trial" |
| **Authority** | People defer to perceived experts and credentials | Author bios, named experts, press, research citations | "Reviewed by [CISSP, CTO at X]" on security content |
| **Liking** | We buy from people (and brands) we like | Show team faces, personality in copy, share failures too | Founder photo on pricing page; honest "where we fall short" section |
| **Unity** | Identity-based appeal — "people like us do things like this" | Niche down the target so the reader sees themselves | "The CRM for solo consultants" beats "CRM for small business" |

## 2. Broader Mental Models for Strategy Decisions

| Model | Short | Marketing Application |
|-------|-------|----------------------|
| **Pareto (80/20)** | 20% of inputs drive 80% of outputs | Find the 20% of pages driving 80% of conversions; ignore the rest |
| **First principles** | Reason from fundamentals, not analogy | Don't copy competitor funnel — ask what *your* user actually needs next |
| **Jobs To Be Done** | People "hire" products to make progress on a job | Map competitor set by the job hired, not by product category |
| **Inversion** | Solve by asking how to achieve the opposite | To boost conversion: list all the ways a page could fail, then fix those |
| **Opportunity cost** | Every choice forecloses alternatives | Publishing that blog post = not doing X; pick the higher-leverage option |
| **Second-order thinking** | What happens *after* what happens? | Aggressive discount now → churn later; free tier → support burden |
| **Theory of constraints** | Throughput is capped by the single biggest bottleneck | Don't optimize email open rate if the bottleneck is landing-page conversion |
| **Diminishing returns** | Each additional unit of input yields less output | Fifth A/B test variant rarely justifies effort; two strong options > ten weak |
| **Barbell strategy** | Avoid the middle; combine extreme safe + extreme risky | 80% of content on proven topics, 20% on bets; no lukewarm "maybe" content |
| **Map ≠ territory** | The model is not reality | Analytics events reflect what you *chose* to track, not what actually happens |
| **Lindy effect** | Things that have lasted tend to keep lasting | Evergreen content compounds; trend-chasing decays |
| **Probabilistic thinking** | Outcomes are distributions, not points | A "good campaign" might be unlucky; one win doesn't prove a strategy |

## 3. Memory & Attention Effects

| Effect | What it does | Application |
|--------|--------------|-------------|
| **Peak-End Rule** | People judge experiences by the peak moment + the ending | Design an emotional peak in onboarding; end every session with a "small win" |
| **Zeigarnik** | Incomplete tasks are remembered more than completed ones | Progress bars ("3/5 steps complete"); "almost done" nudges |
| **Mere Exposure** | Familiarity breeds preference | Retargeting works — even unclicked ads raise conversion on direct visits |
| **Availability Heuristic** | Recent/memorable examples feel more probable | Lead with a specific, named customer story — not aggregate stats |
| **Goal-Gradient** | Effort accelerates near completion | Show "almost there" in checkout; pre-fill fields to reduce perceived distance |
| **IKEA Effect** | People overvalue what they partially built themselves | Let users customize setup, dashboards, onboarding — raises retention |
| **Endowment Effect** | We value what we already have more than equivalent alternatives | Free trials create ownership; cancel flows should highlight what's lost |
| **Pratfall Effect** | Small flaws make competent things more likable | Honest "where we don't fit" section; admitted limitations build trust |
| **Curse of Knowledge** | Experts can't unknow things, making them bad explainers | Test copy on non-users; every industry term should have a plain-English alt |

## 4. Decision-Blocking Biases

These are the ones that *stop* conversions. Name the bias, design around it.

| Bias | Blocks | Fix |
|------|--------|-----|
| **Paradox of Choice** | Too many options → decision paralysis | 3-5 options max; a "most popular" flag cuts through |
| **Status-Quo Bias** | Prefer current state, even when worse | Frame product as the *safe* choice; show "switched from X" stories |
| **Default Effect** | Whatever is pre-selected wins | Set the highest-value plan as default; annual checked by default |
| **Hyperbolic Discounting** | Immediate rewards beat larger delayed ones | "$0 today" > "Save $500/yr"; show today's value first |
| **Sunk Cost Fallacy** | Continuing because of past investment | Cancel flows should remove this: "You keep your data either way" |
| **Zero-Price Effect** | "Free" is psychologically distinct from "cheap" | A $0 tier drives 10x+ signups vs. a $5 tier, even with same revenue math |
| **Confirmation Bias** | Seek evidence for existing beliefs | Lead skeptic users with objection-handling copy before benefits |
| **Fundamental Attribution Error** | Blame the user, not the flow | When users fail, it's almost always the UX — never "users are dumb" |
| **Mimetic Desire** | We want what others want | Lean on competitor switch stories; show named peer customers |

## 5. Ethical Guardrails

These tools can manipulate. The only durable use is alignment — making the choice that's actually good for the user into the easy one.

- **Align incentives**: if the tactic only works when the customer is confused, it's manipulation, not marketing. Confusion causes churn.
- **No fake scarcity**: countdown timers that reset, "only 2 left" when there are 200, fake urgency. One discovery kills trust permanently.
- **Disclosure**: if you're using social proof, the logos and reviews must be real and recent. If using a default, make the alternative findable.
- **Reversibility**: any action nudged by bias should be easy to undo — one-click cancel, no dark-pattern friction on downgrades.
- **Peak-end applies to the whole relationship**: cancel flows and offboarding shape word-of-mouth more than onboarding does. Make them generous.
- **Don't exploit anxiety**: loss aversion is fine; manufactured fear ("your competitors are beating you") is cheap and reads desperate.

> **Heuristic**: If you'd feel weird explaining the tactic to the customer after the sale, it's manipulation. If you'd be proud to show them the playbook, it's marketing.
