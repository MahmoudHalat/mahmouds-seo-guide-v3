# Content Writing Reference

Guidelines for writing content that ranks, converts, and satisfies search intent.

---

## 1. Five-Phase Writing Process

### Phase 1 -- Research (40-60% of total time)
- Search your target keyword incognito; identify what content types rank and what topics they cover
- Determine search intent first -- if you get this wrong, nothing else matters
- Check: Is the user expecting a guide, a comparison, a product page, or a community answer?
- Note what rich media top pages use (images, embedded videos, charts, tools)
- Do top rankers go deep on one subtopic or cover broad ground shallowly?
- Use AI deep research to scan forums, videos, Reddit, discussions simultaneously (~10 min)
- Mine video content for first-hand experiences not found in written sources
- Study book tables of contents for optimal information structure
- Interview experts for perspectives locked away in their minds (even brief text Q&A works)
- For cornerstone content only -- expert interviews significantly expand scope and timeline
- Output: a comprehensive brief covering intent, subtopics, data points, unique angles

### Phase 2 -- Outline
- If research was thorough, the outline flows naturally
- Study competitor outlines for structural patterns; do not copy
- Outline in your writing tool: H1, H2s for each section, H3s for subtopics, notes under each
- Semantic hierarchy: one H1, subtopics as H2s, sub-subtopics as H3s
- A strong outline makes writing feel like paint-by-numbers

### Phase 3 -- Write
- Do not break momentum; get content on the page, refine later
- Open with the answer -- no long preamble about why the topic matters
- For "how much does X cost?" give the price range in the first 10 words
- Each heading = one isolated idea as part of a larger whole; keep copy topically isolated
- Web content should be highly skimmable; use headings judiciously as isolated sections
- Use AI as assistant: check list completeness, find a stat, get a definition
- Do not ask AI to write entire sections; the more you outsource at once, the more voice disappears
- If using AI for drafts, go section by section -- never one-shot an entire post

### Phase 4 -- Edit
- Add missing semantic keywords naturally; check word count against top rankers
- Add internal links to/from 3-5 related pages (internal links pass authority between pages; hugely underutilized)
- Format: short paragraphs (2-4 sentences), bulleted/numbered lists, bold key phrases
- Avoid wall-of-text; ensure plenty of line breaks
- Read content aloud, on your phone, while walking -- reveals repetition, awkward phrasing, and errors
- Delete filler with prejudice
- Check that lists are formatted as actual bulleted or numbered lists, not inline text

### Phase 5 -- Polish (Content Packaging)
- Your content must offer a better experience than a chat response
- Add: expert quotes, embedded videos, charts/diagrams, custom images, callout boxes, animated GIFs
- Preview on staging site in final template context before publishing

## 2. Blog Post Structure Template

```
H1: [Primary keyword + clear topic statement]
Opening (2-3 sentences): State the problem, signal you will answer concretely
H2: [Core answer / main content]
  H3: [Subtopic 1]
  H3: [Subtopic 2]
H2: [Practical examples / case studies] -- at least one with specific numbers
H2: [Step-by-step process] (if applicable)
H2: [Common mistakes] -- 3-5 specific pitfalls
H2: [FAQ] -- 3-5 questions targeting related searches
CTA / Next steps
```

**Keyword placement:**
- Title tag: front-loaded, under 60 chars
- H1: includes primary keyword
- First 100 words: primary keyword appears naturally
- 1-2 H2 headings: keyword or close variations
- Meta description: 120-160 chars, keyword + reason to click
- URL slug: short, readable, keyword-rich (e.g., `/blog/optimize-title-tags`)
- After initial placements: synonyms and natural language only -- no stuffing

**Metadata:**
- Title tag: "[Keyword] -- [Value Prop]" under 60 chars
- Meta description: 120-160 chars, keyword + CTA
- Structured data: `Article` or `BlogPosting`; add `HowTo` for tutorials, `FAQPage` for FAQ sections

## 3. Landing Page Templates

**Universal principles:**
- Specificity converts: replace every vague claim with numbers or examples
- Benefits first, but include granular feature details (AI systems need specific facts to cite you)
- Address objections directly: price, trust, switching costs, "does it work?"
- One page, one job: every element moves toward the CTA
- Social proof is mandatory: testimonials, logos, case studies, stats
- 500-1500 words of genuine content minimum (thin pages with just headline + form do not rank)
- FAQ section serves dual purpose: conversion (objection handling) + SEO (long-tail queries)
- SEO for landing pages: lighter keyword density than blog posts; primary keyword in title, H1, first paragraph, 1 H2, meta description

**Service page:** H1: Service + Benefit > Problem > How It Works (3-4 steps) > Results with numbers > Social Proof > Pricing > FAQ (5-8 objection-handling Qs) > CTA

**Product page:** H1: Product + Benefit > Features as Benefits > How It Works > Who It's For > Reviews > Pricing > FAQ > CTA

**Comparison page:** H1: You vs Competitor > Quick verdict up front > Side-by-side table > Where you win > Where they win (honesty builds trust) > Who should choose which > FAQ > CTA

**Location page:** H1: Service in City > Local-specific content (NOT generic copy with city swapped) > Local case studies > Service Areas > Local FAQ > CTA with local contact

## 4. Content Anti-Patterns

**Structural:** Keyword stuffing | Thin content (<300 words for hard terms) | Wall of text | Duplicate intent (two pages same keyword = cannibalization) | Stale content | Long preamble before the answer | Lazy programmatic pages (city name swapped only)

**AI-specific:** Generic summaries of common knowledge | Excessive hedging without positions | Missing experience signals | Fabricated statistics | M-dashes/buzzwords/corporate tone | Topic drift mid-section | Benefits listed without evidence

**Strategic:** Publishing new posts while existing content decays | Targeting keywords above your authority | Scaling AI content at volume | Self-promotional listicles on your own domain (site reputation abuse risk)

## 5. Content Quality Checklist

### Must-Have
- [ ] Title tag with primary keyword, under 60 chars
- [ ] Meta description with keyword + CTA, 120-160 chars
- [ ] Single H1 with primary keyword
- [ ] Logical heading hierarchy (H1 > H2 > H3)
- [ ] Primary keyword in first 100 words naturally
- [ ] Internal links to 3-5 related pages with descriptive anchor text
- [ ] Images with descriptive alt text
- [ ] Short readable URL with keyword slug
- [ ] At least one specific example, case study, or data point
- [ ] FAQ section (3-5 questions targeting related searches)

### Should-Have
- [ ] Structured data (Article, Product, Service, HowTo, FAQPage as appropriate)
- [ ] Table of contents for content over 1500 words
- [ ] External links to authoritative sources for factual claims
- [ ] Author byline with relevant credentials
- [ ] Last-updated date for evergreen content
- [ ] Embedded video, chart, or custom image
- [ ] Expert quote or original data point

### Pre-Publish
- [ ] Read content aloud on phone
- [ ] All internal/external links verified
- [ ] Mobile preview checked
- [ ] Structured data validates

## 6. The "Last Click" Test

After reading, would the reader need to search again? If yes, content is not done.
- Satisfy the primary desire in the opening
- Then answer the next five questions the reader would logically have
- Include practical next steps so the reader knows what to do after reading
- AI overviews filter out casual searchers; your job is to be indispensable for the serious ones

---

## 7. Humanizing AI-Assisted Content

**Keep AI on a short leash:** Ask for one definition, one list check, one stat. Use partially or completely while retaining your style. Entire sections from AI = voice erasure. Entire posts from AI = generic content.

**What requires the human:** Search intent judgment for your niche | Product knowledge and real usage context | Voice and personality | Taking clear positions | Specific examples from real experience | Deciding the best content angle

**Experience-driven content:** Run experiments, take swings, build something ambitious. Share the wins, failures, and lessons. This layer cannot be copied. Live the story only you can tell.

**Editing for humanness:** Remove M-dashes, buzzwords, corporate phrasing | Replace hedging with clear positions | Add specific numbers, dates, names | Inject personality and opinions | Every section must contain something only a practitioner would know

## 8. Conversion Copy Patterns (v3)

Sections 1-7 cover blog/article prose. This section covers conversion copy — pages where the goal is to move the reader toward action, not inform. Landing pages, CTAs, hero sections, pricing anxiety relief, about pages.

### CTA formula

`[Action Verb] + [What They Get] + [Optional Qualifier]`

Good examples:
- "Start your free trial" (action + qualifier)
- "Get your pricing quote" (action + what)
- "See how it works — no signup" (action + what + qualifier)
- "Book a 15-min demo" (action + qualifier)
- "Download the 2026 report" (action + what)
- "Try it free for 14 days" (action + qualifier)

Avoid: "Learn more" (no value), "Click here" (no context), "Submit" (no reward), "Get started" (no specifics unless you're a household name).

### Headline formulas

| Pattern | Example |
|---------|---------|
| `{Outcome} without {pain}` | "Ship clean code without writing tests you don't have time for" |
| `The {category} for {specific audience}` | "The CRM for solo consultants" |
| `Never {unpleasant task} again` | "Never track expenses manually again" |
| `{Provocative question that highlights pain}` | "Why are your reports still in Excel?" |
| `{Number} + {benefit} + {timeframe}` | "37% more replies in 30 days" |
| `Stop {bad outcome}, start {good outcome}` | "Stop guessing, start shipping features users want" |

Strong headlines do two jobs at once: they name the target audience AND the outcome. "The CRM for solo consultants" tells a solo consultant they're in the right place AND implies outcome (something built for how they actually work).

### Page-type focus

| Page | Primary job | Common pitfall |
|------|-------------|----------------|
| Homepage | Answer "what is this and who is it for" in 5 seconds | Speaking to everyone → speaking to no one |
| Landing page | Move ONE audience toward ONE action | Multiple CTAs competing for attention |
| Pricing | Self-select + "which is right for me?" + anxiety relief | Hiding prices; too many tiers; no guidance |
| Feature page | Show the job done, not the feature | Feature description without a use-case narrative |
| About page | Build trust via story + team + values | Corporate bio with zero personality |
| Comparison page | Honest differentiation | Obvious self-flattery; strawmanning competitors |

### The "So What" test

Every feature bullet needs a benefit bridge. Pattern:

`Feature → "which means..." → Benefit → "so you can..." → Outcome`

Examples:
- Real-time collaboration → which means everyone sees edits instantly → so you can stop emailing files back and forth
- Webhook support → which means any change triggers your workflow → so you can automate reporting without engineering
- Single sign-on → which means no new passwords for your team → so you can onboard in one click from your IdP

If a feature bullet doesn't finish the "so you can..." sentence with something the reader actually cares about, it's filler.

### Prove-it gaps (common conversion copy sins)

- Unsubstantiated claims ("fastest," "industry-leading," "the best") without evidence
- Benefits listed without proof points (numbers, named customer quotes, logos)
- Generic testimonials (no role, no company, no specificity) — "John, happy customer" is worse than no testimonial
- "Used by thousands" without named customers or logos
- CTAs followed by no risk reducer ("no credit card required," "free forever," "14-day money back") when reader is high-anxiety
- Feature pages that describe the feature without showing the feature (need a screenshot or short clip)
- Comparison tables with all green checkmarks on your side and all red on theirs (reads as biased, kills trust)

### Risk reducers by page type

| Page | Common anxiety | Reducer |
|------|----------------|---------|
| Signup | "What will this cost me?" | "No credit card required" / "Free forever plan" |
| Pricing | "Am I locked in?" | "Cancel anytime" / "Monthly billing available" |
| Trial | "Will I lose my data?" | "Export everything anytime" / "Your data is yours" |
| Demo request | "Is this going to be a sales trap?" | "15 minutes, no sales pitch" / "Walk away with your answer" |
| Enterprise | "Is this secure enough?" | SOC 2, ISO, GDPR, HIPAA badges + link to security page |
